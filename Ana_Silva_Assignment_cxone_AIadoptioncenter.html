Research → What is changing

The industry is moving from a traditional software development lifecycle to an AI-assisted one. The main product opportunity is not to start with enforcement, but to first make AI participation visible and understandable.

Before: traditional development

DeveloperWrites code and selects dependencies.
→
Security checksTools scan code, packages, containers, IaC and secrets.

Now: AI-assisted development

Developer + AIAssistants, agents and external models help create, change and connect code.
→
More unknownsOrganizations may not know which AI is used, what it can access, or what it changed.
1. Visibility gap

Teams may use AI tools that the company does not know about, making it difficult to assess risk or support responsible adoption.

2. Trust gap

AI can suggest code, packages or actions, but organizations need a way to understand whether those suggestions come from approved and trustworthy sources.

3. Governance gap

Security and engineering leaders need shared visibility before they can define policies, guide teams or introduce automation.

Product vision

Enable organizations to confidently adopt AI in software development by making AI usage visible, understandable and, over time, governable.

The first milestone is visibility. Governance, trust scoring and automation should come after the organization understands its AI landscape.

What’s Next → Simple roadmap

🎯

Phase 2 — Trust Score & guardrails

The next priority is to transform visibility into a simple, explainable score that helps teams understand whether their AI usage is moving in the right direction.

  • Policy setup: customers define lightweight rules first, such as approved AI tools, blocked tools, required review for unknown tools, and repositories that need stricter controls.
  • Score inputs: approved tool usage, repository coverage, classification status, policy alignment and unresolved review items.
  • Example score: a team starts at 72/100. It improves when most repositories use approved AI tools and all detected tools are classified. It drops when unknown tools appear, blocked tools are used, or sensitive repositories are missing review.
  • Primary score level: team / repository score. This is safer and more useful for management than ranking individual developers.
  • Developer view: developers see guidance on what improves the score, for example “use approved tools” or “request review for this AI assistant.”
  • Management view: security and engineering leaders see team trends, high-risk areas and progress over time.
